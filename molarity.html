Molarity

Molarity, also called molar concentration, is the amount of moles of solute in a certain volume of solution.

Standard Formula: M = n/V

Where:

  • M = Molarity/Concentration (mol/L)
  • n = Moles of solute (mol)
  • V = Volume of solution (L)

Practice: Finding Concentration

Calculate the concentration of a solution that contains 0.5 moles of solute in 2 liters of solution.

Using the formula \( M = \frac{n}{V} \), we can substitute the given values:

\( M = \frac{0.5 \, \text{mol}}{2 \, \text{L}} \)

\( M = 0.25 \, \text{mol/L} \)

So, the concentration of the solution is 0.25 mol/L.

Practice: Finding Moles

Calculate the number of moles of solute in a solution with a concentration of 0.25 mol/L and a volume of 2 liters.

Using the formula \( n = M \times V \), we can substitute the given values:

\( n = 0.25 \, \text{mol/L} \times 2 \, \text{L} \)

\( n = 0.5 \, \text{mol} \)

So, the number of moles of solute in the solution is 0.5 mol.

Practice: Finding Volume

Calculate the volume of a solution that contains 0.5 moles of solute and has a concentration of 0.25 mol/L.

Using the formula \( V = \frac{n}{M} \), we can substitute the given values:

\( V = \frac{0.5 \, \text{mol}}{0.25 \, \text{mol/L}} \)

\( V = 2 \, \text{L} \)

So, the volume of the solution is 2 L.

Practice: Finding concentration with given mass and volume

An aqueous 0.300 M glucose solution is prepared with a total volume of 0.150 L. What mass of glucose (in grams) is needed for the solution?

First we need to find the number of moles of glucose:

\( n = M \times V \)

\( n = 0.300 \, \text{mol/L} \times 0.150 \, \text{L} \)

\( n = 0.045 \, \text{mol} \)

Next, we convert moles to mass using the molecular weight:

\( \text{mass} = n \times \text{molecular weight} \)

\( \text{mass} = 0.045 \, \text{mol} \times 180.16 \, \dfrac{\text{g}}{\text{mol}} \)

\( \text{mass} = 8.1072 \, \text{g} \)

So, the mass of glucose needed is 8.11 g.
