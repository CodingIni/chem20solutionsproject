Molarity

Molarity, also called molar concentration, is the amount of moles of solute in a certain volume of solution.

Standard Formula: C = n/V

Where:

  • C = Molarity/Concentration (mol/L)
  • n = Moles of solute (mol)
  • V = Volume of solution (L)

Practice: Finding Concentration

Calculate the concentration of a solution that contains 0.5 moles of solute in 2 liters of solution.

The formula is: \( C = \frac{n}{V} \)

$$C = \frac{0.5 \, \text{mol}}{2 \, \text{L}}$$

\( C = 0.25 \, \text{mol/L} \)

The concentration of the solution is 0.25 mol/L.

Practice: Finding Moles

Determine the number of moles of KOH present in 95.0 mL of a 0.255 C solution.

Manipulating the formula for \( n \), we get:

$$n = C \times V$$

\( n = 0.255 \, \text{mol/L} \times 0.0950 \, \text{L} \)


\( n = 0.024225 \, \text{mol} \)

The number of moles of solute in the solution is \( 2.42 \times 10^{-2} \) mol.

Practice: Finding Volume

What is the volume of a 0.1 C HCl solution containing 0.04 moles of HCL?.

Manipulating the formula for \( V \), we get:

$$V = \frac{n}{C}$$

$$V = \frac{0.04 \, \text{mol}}{0.1 \, \text{mol/L}}$$

\( V = 2.0 \, \text{L} \)

The volume of the solution is 2.0 L.

Practice: Finding concentration with given mass and volume

Find the concentration of a solution containing 0.515 g of H2SO4 in 1.00 L of solution

First we need to find the number of moles of H2SO4 in the solution:

\( n = \frac{\text{mass}}{\text{molar mass}} \)

\( n = \frac{0.515 \, \text{g}}{98.08 \,{\text{g}}/{\text{mol}}} \)

\( n = 0.00525 \, \text{mol} \)


Then we find the concentration using the mass and the volume:

\( C = \frac{n}{V} \)

\( C = \frac{0.00525 \, \text{mol}}{1.00 \, \text{L}} \)

\( C = 0.00525 \, \text{mol/L} \)

The concentration of the solution is 5.25 × 10⁻³ mol/L.

Practice: Finding mass with given concentration and volume

A 0.300 M glucose solution is prepared with a total volume of 0.150 L. What mass of glucose (in grams) is needed for the solution?

First we need to find the number of moles of glucose in the solution:

\( n = M \times V \)

\( n = 0.300 \, \text{mol/L} \times 0.150 \, \text{L} \)

\( n = 0.045 \, \text{mol} \)


Next, we convert moles to mass using the molecular weight:

\( \text{mass} = n \times \text{molecular weight} \)

\( \text{mass} = 0.045 \, \text{mol} \times 180.16 \, \dfrac{\text{g}}{\text{mol}} \)

\( \text{mass} = 8.1072 \, \text{g} \)

The mass of glucose needed is 8.11 g.
